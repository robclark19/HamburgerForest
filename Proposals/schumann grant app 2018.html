General Criteria, and Limitations

  • Qualifying tax-exempt 501(c)(3) organizations are eligible to apply for grants consistent with the purposes of the foundation to which they are applying.
  • Applicants that have previously received funds must be in good standing with respect to any required status reports or other conditions imposed before submitting a new proposal.

  • If a grant is approved, the grantee may be required to sign and return a grant agreement prior to receiving funds.
  • Progress reports and completion reports may also be required for approved grants.

Common goals   
 
 Describe how a grant to your organization would further the foundation's mission and the priorities of the foundation.  

 		Great Hollow Nature Preserve & Ecological Research Center is a newly-founded 501(c) 3 non-profit organization that is dedicated to wildlife conservation, applied ecological research, experiential environmental education, and outdoor recreational opportunities for the public enjoyment of nature. At the core of our mission is innovative conservation-driven science. The proposed project will allow us to acquire the first empirical data on whether the spread of the non-native and highly invasive plant, Japanese barberry, throughout the forests of the eastern U.S. is affecting biodiversity and habitat quality for birds and other wildlife. The results will allow for more science-based decision-making among land managers and conservation practitioners who are grappling with Japanese barberry invasions. Combating Japanese barberry on a large scale is extremely labor intensive and costly, and the effects of Japanese barberry and other non-native plants on wildlife need to be better understood before allocating limited conservation resources to their eradication.

Request detail
Request detail
 
Describe the objectives of the project or program to be funded.   
 

 		Japanese barberry has quickly become one of the most widespread non-native invasive plants in the forests of New York and southern New England, and like most other non-native invasive plants, is assumed to significantly diminish ecological integrity. Yet, surprisingly few studies have actually investigated the effects of Japanese barberry on wildlife. Our goals are to acquire the first empirical data on whether the spread of Japanese barberry through the forests of the Northeast is negatively affecting wildlife diversity and habitat quality. Our study will be highly integrative (combining ecology and physiology) and based upon several indicators that span multiple animal taxa, including songbirds, salamanders, and terrestrial invertebrates. Combating Japanese barberry on a large scale is extremely labor intensive and costly, and the effects of Japanese barberry and other non-native plants on wildlife need to be better understood before allocating limited conservation resources to their eradication.




 
 
Describe the implementation plan for the project or program. Include at least three specific actions your organization will take in order to achieve results.   
 

 		Forests will be identified in Putnam County, NY and Fairfield County, CT that have clusters of barberry in some areas and no barberry in others. Plots will be established in areas with dense or little to no barberry coverage, and between them we will compare the following to assess the effects of barberry on birds as well as salamanders and invertebrates: (1) physiological condition indices, nest success, and migration success (determined by cutting edge nano-tags; see www.motus.org) of ovenbirds; (2) bird diversity (determined by "citizen scientist" point-count surveys); (3) invertebrate biomass and diversity on barberry and on the forest floor; and (4) biomass and energetic costs (as a function of body mass, skin temperature, and ground temperature and humidity) of redbacked salamanders. We will also determine the quality (energy, protein, and antioxidant content) of barberry berries as food for birds relative to the berries of native plants.




 
 
What criteria does (or will) your organization use to measure the success of the project or program?   
 
 (Example: Of the 400+ persons we serve/reach, we anticipate that approximately 200 will benefit in the manner described below, whereas without our program no more than 50 might reasonably have been expected to achieve the same result.  		One way in which we will measure success will be the number of natural resources management agencies and organizations to which we can disseminate the results of our study. A report entitled "How is Japanese Barberry Affecting Wildlife Communities on Your Lands?" will be prepared on the basis of our study's findings and distributed to these agencies and organizations throughout the Northeast. Publication of the study's findings in a peer-reviewed science journal and presentation of the findings at professional conferences will also be primary benchmarks of success. Another anticipated success of the project will be the ability to provide paid field assistant positions to undergraduate students or early career professionals in which they will gain valuable field biology experience, while also providing opportunities for "citizen scientists" from our local communities to participate in an authentic research experience.




 
 
Collaboration   
 
 Briefly describe any formal or informal collaborative ventures your organization has established (or will establish)
with other entities serving similar purposes that may be relevant to this grant request.		  

 		Dr. Susan Smith, Assistant Professor at the Rochester Institute of Technology (Rochester, NY), will be a collaborator on this project. Dr. Smith and her students will provide in-kind support by analyzing multiple parameters ("condition indices") in the blood samples that will be collected from birds in areas with and without barberry. These include levels of triglyceride and total plasma protein, and heterophil:lymphocyte ratios. Dr. Smith will also analyze the quality (energy, protein, and antioxidant content) of barberry berries as food for birds and other wildlife relative to berries produced by native plants.

Dr. Scott Williams of the Connecticut Agricultural Experimental Station will be contacted to find out if he may wish to collaborate on the project in some capacity. Dr. Williams has extensive experience studying the effectiveness of barberry eradication methods and has also studied the effects of barberry on soil microclimate, tick density, and Lyme disease prevalence in Connecticut.
